Building red-teaming capacity for AI safety evaluation in African contexts

Frontier AI models routinely produce harmful outputs in low-resource languages that they refuse in English. This workshop is a working introduction to the techniques used to find these failures — applied to your own languages, in a small group, in a single afternoon.

About

A working introduction to red-teaming, taught for African languages and contexts.

AI systems are being deployed across African contexts, in local languages, in domains where errors carry real consequences. The evaluation infrastructure to assess their safety has not kept pace. Safety benchmarks remain predominantly English-language and derived from harm taxonomies developed outside Africa.

Red-teaming — the systematic, adversarial probing of a model to surface harmful, unsafe, or misaligned outputs — is one of the primary methodologies used by AI safety institutes to identify these failures. It is a learnable, replicable skill, and it is largely absent from the African AI research community.

This workshop teaches a focused set of red-teaming techniques you can apply right away. We're not attempting a survey of the full discipline in three hours — instead, you'll leave with a working foundation in a few core techniques, applied to your own languages, with a clear sense of where to go deeper afterwards.

The exercise

What you'll actually do in the room.

You'll join a group of four to six people, mixing technical and policy researchers. Your group is assigned one harm domain — say, medical misinformation, political content, or sensitive personal advice.

You'll work through a structured red-teaming interface on your laptop, with a facilitation guide that includes the threat model and two annotated examples. Your task: construct adversarial prompts in at least two languages, test them against a live model, and classify each response.

Facilitators circulate to coach, push your prompt construction beyond first-draft formulations, and surface cross-lingual asymmetries you might have missed. At the end, groups present their strongest prompts and what the model actually did.

What to bring. A laptop is required — you'll use it to access the red-teaming interface, test prompts against a live model, and record your results. Wi-Fi will be available at the venue.

If you don't have a laptop you can bring, get in touch at info@casa-ai.org before the workshop and we'll see what we can arrange.

No other materials needed. Facilitation guides and threat-model cards are provided.

Who it's for

Designed for a mixed audience of technical and policy researchers.

Come if you work with AI systems in African languages, or you think about how AI affects African communities. No prior red-teaming experience is required — the workshop teaches it from scratch.

  • AI safety or NLP researchers working in low-resource or African-language settings.
  • Policy or social-science researchers working on AI governance, harm, or accountability in Africa.
  • Civil society or applied researchers evaluating AI tools being deployed in African contexts.
  • PhD and postdoctoral researchers looking for a methodological frame for safety-related work.
Not for you if…

You're looking for an introduction to large language models, or a tutorial on fine-tuning. We assume basic familiarity with how LLMs work; we don't assume any safety-specific background.

Responsible conduct

The exercise teaches a method, not how to generate harm.

Red-teaming involves probing AI systems for unsafe behaviour. The session is designed so you build the skill without being asked to produce content that's distressing to generate or read. Materials are prepared in advance, facilitators are briefed on the protocol, and participation is voluntary (18+).

You'll be asked for written consent at the start. No personally identifying information is collected; prompts are documented without attribution and never reproduced verbatim in research outputs. Whether your contributions feed into the Africa AI Red-Teaming Framework dataset is fully opt-in.

FAQ

Common questions.

Do I need to know how to red-team already?

No. The workshop teaches it from scratch. We're not assuming any safety-specific background — just basic familiarity with how LLMs work.

Will I leave able to red-team independently?

You'll leave with a working foundation — a focused set of techniques and the methodological frame to apply them. Red-teaming as a full discipline is broader than what we can cover in three hours, and we're upfront about that. The workshop is a starting point, not your only training.

Will I be asked to generate harmful content?

No. You'll construct adversarial prompts in a structured way, on prepared topics, with facilitator support. The goal is to learn the methodology, not to produce shock material.

Will we test against a real AI model?

Yes. You'll work through a red-teaming interface that connects to a live model so you can see how it actually responds. The interface is provided; you just need to bring a laptop.

I work in policy and don't code — is this for me?

Yes. The hands-on exercise is explicitly designed for mixed technical-and-policy groups. Prompt construction needs your contextual knowledge as much as it needs technical instinct.

Is the content I produce used in research?

Only with your written consent. Prompts can be contributed to the Africa AI Red-Teaming Framework dataset; contributors are acknowledged. You can also opt out and keep your work to yourself.

Will there be a recording?

No. The workshop is in-person only.
