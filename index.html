Building red-teaming capacity for AI safety evaluation in African contexts

Format
Interactive workshop
Duration
Three hours
Capacity
Up to 40 participants
Audience
Technical & policy researchers
About

A practical introduction to the methodology used to evaluate AI safety — taught for African languages and contexts.

AI systems are being deployed at scale across African contexts, in local languages, across varied sociopolitical environments, and in domains where errors carry real consequences. The evaluation infrastructure to assess their safety has not kept pace. Safety benchmarks remain predominantly English-language and derived from harm taxonomies developed outside Africa.

Red-teaming — the systematic, adversarial probing of a model to surface harmful, unsafe, or misaligned outputs — is one of the primary methodologies used by AI safety institutes to identify these failures. It is a learnable, replicable skill. And it is largely absent from the African AI research community.

This workshop teaches that skill. Over three hours, participants work through the full red-teaming methodology: they learn the protocol through a structured presentation and a live worked example, then apply it in a hands-on group exercise. The session is practical by design — participants leave having completed a red-teaming sequence themselves.

Why now

Three reasons the African AI community needs this capacity now.

i.

To evaluate AI systems on its own terms

Frontier AI systems are being deployed across African languages and contexts without safety evaluation conducted by people with the relevant contextual knowledge. Red-teaming is one of the primary tools for that evaluation. A research community that cannot do it is dependent on others to assess whether the AI systems affecting its communities are safe.

ii.

The skill is context-dependent in ways that matter

Red-teaming in African contexts is not the same as red-teaming in English. The same prompt behaves differently across languages. Harm categories are culturally situated. A guardrail that works in English may fail in Swahili, Hausa, or Zulu. Building this capacity requires researchers who understand those languages and contexts — meaning it must be built here.

iii.

Capacity built in this session compounds

Participants who leave able to run a red-teaming session can teach it in their institutions and apply it in their research. The workshop is designed as a starting point for ongoing community practice, not a standalone event.

Programme

The session moves from framing, to method, to practice.

Part I
Setting the scene
An opening that frames the problem, followed by a panel of researchers working on AI evaluation, safety, and African language AI. The panel grounds the methodology in the problems it is designed to address.
00:00 – 00:45
45 min
Part II
The method
A step-by-step walkthrough of the red-teaming protocol — moving from a concern, to a threat model, to adversarial prompts, to a classification of failures — followed by a live worked example run end-to-end in two languages.
00:45 – 01:30
45 min
Part III
Hands-on exercise
Small mixed groups of technical and policy researchers apply the methodology to an assigned harm domain. Each group constructs a minimum of five adversarial prompts in at least two languages or registers, annotated with the failure mode targeted. Facilitators circulate and coach. A short debrief draws out cross-group themes.
01:30 – 02:30
60 min
Part IV
Close
Next steps — how participants can apply this methodology in their own institutions, how to engage with the Africa AI Red-Teaming Framework, and an open invitation to collaborate on the research outputs.
02:30 – 02:45
15 min
Take away

A replicable methodology you can apply, teach, and extend.

01

The full red-teaming loop

You will be able to move from a safety concern to a structured threat model, to a set of adversarial prompts, to a classification of the failures observed.

02

A cross-lingual lens

You will understand why the same prompt behaves differently across languages, and how to design adversarial tests that surface these asymmetries deliberately.

03

Hands-on experience

You will have completed a red-teaming sequence yourself, with facilitator support, before leaving the room.

04

A path to continue

You leave connected to a cohort of researchers working on the same problems, with a clear route into the Africa AI Red-Teaming Framework and its outputs.

Audience

Designed for a mixed audience of technical and policy researchers.

Red-teaming in African contexts requires both technical researchers, who can construct and evaluate adversarial prompts, and policy and social-science researchers, who understand the harm categories, cultural contexts, and sociopolitical dynamics that make a failure meaningful. The session is structured so that each part is accessible and valuable to both groups, and the hands-on exercise is explicitly designed for mixed groups.

No prior red-teaming experience is required. An interest in how AI systems behave in your language and context is the only prerequisite.

Responsible conduct

The exercise is structured to teach a method, not to generate harm.

Red-teaming necessarily involves probing AI systems for unsafe behaviour. The session is designed so that participants build this skill without being asked to generate content that would be distressing to produce or encounter. Facilitation materials are prepared in advance by the organising team.

  • Participant information
    The workshop description published in advance states clearly what the exercise involves. Participation is voluntary; an age requirement of 18 or above applies.
  • Written consent
    Participants provide written consent at the start of the session covering participation in the exercise and any use of materials produced.
  • Facilitator oversight
    All facilitators are briefed in advance on the protocol and on procedures for redirecting group work if needed.
  • Data handling
    No personally identifiable information is collected. Prompts are documented without attribution. Research outputs will not reproduce detailed adversarial prompts verbatim.

    Indaba theme

    Sovereign intelligence, in practice, is the ability to evaluate.

    The 2026 Indaba theme — Sovereign Intelligence: Africa's Path in a Frontier AI World — invites a forward-looking question: how can African communities meaningfully shape and govern the AI systems that affect them? This workshop engages that question through a focused, practical lens: strengthening the capacity to evaluate whether AI systems are safe, reliable, and aligned with African contexts.

    Sovereign intelligence, in practice, is about building the ability to understand, assess, and shape AI systems within one's own context. This workshop contributes to that vision by developing a core component of that capacity: red-teaming as a rigorous, hands-on methodology for probing system behaviour. Participants apply these techniques directly to African-language settings, advancing both practical skills and shared approaches within the research community.

    Outputs

    What the workshop leaves behind.

    Participant skills

    The primary output. Each participant leaves with a replicable red-teaming methodology they can apply independently — to evaluate models in their own language contexts, to integrate into research projects, or to teach within their institutions.

    Public summary piece

    A synthesis document covering the methodology presented, key themes from the debrief, and anonymised insights from the exercise. Published and shared with the wider Indaba community following the event.

    Community connections

    A cohort of African AI researchers with a shared methodology, connected to each other and to the Africa AI Red-Teaming Framework. Participants who wish to collaborate on the framework will be invited to do so.
